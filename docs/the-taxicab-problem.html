3 The Taxicab Problem

3.1 Green Cab/Blue Cab

Here’s a simple-seeming problem that goes back to 1972, and ultimately led to a Nobel prize 30 years later, in 2002. (This particular version is from 1982.)

A cab was involved in a hit and run accident at night. Two cab companies, the Green and the Blue, operate in the city. You are given the following data:

  1. 85% of the cabs in the city are Green and 15% are Blue.
  2. A witness identified the cab as Blue. The court tested the reliability of the witness under the same circumstances that existed on the night of the accident and concluded that the witness correctly identified each one of the two colors 80% of the time and failed 20% of the time.

What is the probability that the cab involved in the accident was Blue rather than Green?

The most common answer is 80%. After all, the witness is right 80% of the time, right? But it wouldn’t be a famous problem if the obvious answer were the right one.

The right answer is 12/29, or about 41%.

Why? Well Green cabs are way more common. They’re 85% of the taxis in town. So even if the witness is right 80% of the time, they’ll mistakenly say “Blue” when it’s really Green pretty often. 20% of those 85% of cases are going to mistaken “Blue” reports. Compared with only 80% of the 15% cases where the cab really is Blue.
