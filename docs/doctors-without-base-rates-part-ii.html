4.3 Doctors Without Base Rates, part II

In our discussion of the base rate fallacy, we saw how to account for base rates correctly using Bayes’ theorem:

\[ Pr(H \given E) = Pr(H)\frac{Pr(E \given H)}{Pr(E)}. \]

We also discussed the following, counterintuitive example:

About 1 in every 100 people have Weisberg’s syndrome (an annoying but mostly harmless disease). There’s a blood test that’s 90% accurate: in 90% of cases where the disease is present, the test comes up positive, and in 90% of cases where it’s absent the test comes up negative.

Suppose a randomly selected person tests positive. What are the chances they have the disease?

Using a picture, we estimated the answer was about 1/11. Now let’s use Bayes’ theorem to solve this problem exactly:

  • We want to calculate \(Pr(D \given P)\), the probability of having the disease given a positive blood test. Bayes’ theorem says: \[ Pr(D \given P) = Pr(D) \frac{Pr(P \given D)}{Pr(P)}.\]
  • So to apply Bayes’ theorem, we need three numbers:
    • \(Pr(D) = 1/100\), that’s the base rate given in the problem.
    • \(Pr(P \given D) = 90/100\), that’s the test-accuracy given.
    • \(Pr(P) = \ldots\) uh-oh this number isn’t given to us.
  • You guessed it, LTP to the rescue! \[ \begin{aligned} Pr(P) &= Pr(P \given D)Pr(D) + Pr(P \given \neg D)Pr(\neg D)\\ &= (90/100)(1/100) + (10/100)(99/100)\\ &= 1,080/10,000\\ &= 27/250. \end{aligned} \]
  • So our final answer is: \[ \begin{aligned} Pr(D \given P) &= 1/100 \frac{90/100}{27/250}\\ &= 0.0833\overline{3}. \end{aligned} \]

Moral: Bayes’ theorem and the LTP make a powerful combination. In fact, you’ll often see Bayes’ theorem written with the LTP built in: \[ Pr(H \given E) = \frac{Pr(E \given H)Pr(H)}{ Pr(E \given H) Pr(H) + Pr(E \given \neg H)Pr(\neg H)}.\] But… ew.
