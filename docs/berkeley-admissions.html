4 Berkeley Admissions

In 1973, the University of California at Berkeley got worried about being sued for discriminating against women in their graduate admissions. Only 35% of female applicants had been admitted compared with 44% of male applicants.

Peter Bickel, a professor in the statistics department, was recruited to look into it. He found that men and women actually had roughly the same acceptance rates department by department. In fact, women had a higher acceptance rate across most of the larger departments.

How could that be? Women had applied more to the selective departments, men to the less selective departments. Many more women than men had applied to the English department, for example, whereas more men had applied to Mechanical Engineering. And English admitted a much lower portion of applicants than Mechanical Engineering.

That doesn’t mean sexism played no role here. You might be suspicious, for example, about why women and men differed so much in their choices of where to apply. And just because one group does better in an admissions process doesn’t mean they aren’t being discriminated against. Harvard once had an admissions cap on Jews because they were so overrepresented!

But it does mean the initial appearance of discrimination in the Berkeley process was misleading. And it’s a great illustration of Simpson’s paradox: a trend can appear at one level of analysis yet be reversed at a finer-grained level.

In fact, it’s even possible for one group to have higher admissions across the board, in every department, and yet have a lower admission rate overall. Imagine a fictional case with just two departments. And let’s combine our two bar charts by using width to display the number of applicants:

The moral: there’s a part-whole fallacy to watch out for with probabilities. Just because a trend is present in every slice of a population doesn’t mean it’ll be present in the population overall.
