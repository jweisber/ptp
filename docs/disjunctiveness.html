6.3 Disjunctiveness

Here’s one very natural and appealing reply. The problem with grue is that it’s a disjunctive concept: it’s defined using EITHER/OR.

Part of the beauty of Goodman’s puzzle, however, is that it comes with a neat way of exposing the flaw in this diagnosis. Because it allows us to make ‘green’ the disjunctive concept in stead.

How? Let’s start by building grue a friend, a concept to fill in the missing spaces in our original diagram. We’ll call it “been”:

To make the definition explicit:

Bleen

An object is bleen if EITHER (a) it is blue and first observed before the year 2020, OR (b) it’s green and not observed before 2020.

Now imagine some bizarre species of alien lands on Earth, and they don’t have concepts like green and blue. Instead they think, and speak, in terms of grue and bleen.

They hear us talking green/blue talk, and they want to know what it’s all about. How would we tell them? Much the same way they would have to explain grue and bleen to us. We would provide them an EITHER/OR definition, in terms of the grue/bleen language they’re familiar with.
